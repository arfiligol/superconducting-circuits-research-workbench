# Why does nonlinearity make the energy level spacing inconsistent?

## Questions answered in this chapter

Why does the Josephson nonlinearity in superconducting circuits make the energy level difference between `|0\rangle\to|1\rangle` and `|1\rangle\to|2\rangle` no longer the same?

## Prerequisite knowledge comparison

- Quantum: quantized oscillator, perturbation concept
- Classical: Potential energy curvature and small oscillations
- Electromagnetism: capacitive energy and inductive energy

## Physics Core

Linear LC oscillators approximate quadratic potential energy, so the energy levels are equally spaced. Josephson element potential energy

$$
U_J(\varphi) = -E_J\cos\varphi
$$

After expansion near the equilibrium point, in addition to the quadratic terms, there are also fourth-order and higher-order terms, forming an anharmonic oscillator:

$$
U(\varphi) \approx \frac{1}{2}k\varphi^2 + \alpha\varphi^4 + \cdots
$$

The non-quadratic terms make the energy corrections of different quantum numbers different, so the energy level spacing is no longer consistent (anhamonicity).

<Aside type="note" title="Historical context">

Discernible non-equally spaced energy levels allow superconducting circuits to be used as controllable two-energy level systems, which is one of the cores of the feasibility of superconducting qubits.

</Aside>

## Project mapping

- Non-equal spacing determines the selective driving capability of the qubit.
- If the nonlinearity is too small, it will cause leakage; if it is too large, it may increase sensitivity and noise coupling.

## Limitations and approximations

- Small amplitude expansion is only true near the equilibrium point.
- High-order terms and multi-energy level effects need to be considered under strong driving.
