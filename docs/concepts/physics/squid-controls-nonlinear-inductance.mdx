# Why can SQUID control nonlinear inductance values?

## Questions answered in this chapter

Why can the equivalent inductance and nonlinear strength of SQUID be changed only by applying an external magnetic flux bias under a fixed geometric structure?

## Prerequisite knowledge comparison

- Quantum: phase difference and single value of wave function
- Electromagnetic: external magnetic flux and loop constraints
- Classical: equivalent parameters and small signal linearization

## Physics Core

A symmetric SQUID can be written as the effective Josephson energy under common approximations

$$
E_J^{\mathrm{eff}}(\Phi_{\mathrm{ext}}) \approx 2E_{J0}\cos\left(\pi\frac{\Phi_{\mathrm{ext}}}{\Phi_0}\right).
$$

The Josephson inductance is approximately inversely proportional to the energy scale, so the equivalent inductance can be continuously controlled by `\Phi_{\mathrm{ext}}`:

$$
L_J^{\mathrm{eff}} \propto \frac{1}{E_J^{\mathrm{eff}}}.
$$

<Aside type="note" title="Historical context">

Tunable inductors allow a single chip to switch between different operating points, leading to the rapid development of tunable qubits, parametric amplifiers and reconfigurable coupling architectures.

</Aside>

## Project mapping

- Adjustable frequency, nonlinearity and coupling strength using flux bias.
- Design requires a trade-off between adjustable range, sensitivity and noise.

## Limitations and approximations

- When symmetrical and small loops approximate failure, asymmetry and self-induction effects need to be included.
- Near the `E_J^{\mathrm{eff}} \to 0` region, the model is more sensitive to noise and higher-order effects.
