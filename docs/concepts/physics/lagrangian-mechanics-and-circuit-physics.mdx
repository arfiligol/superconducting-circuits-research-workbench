# How is Lagrangian mechanics combined with circuit physics?

## Questions answered in this chapter

Why can we write a circuit system into the same Lagrangian as a particle system and further quantize it?

## Prerequisite knowledge comparison

- Classical: Lagrange equation and generalized coordinates
- Electromagnetic: capacitive energy storage and inductive energy storage
- Quantum: regular quantization

## Physics Core

Under the nodal flux variable `\phi_i`, a common form is

$$
\mathcal{L}(\{\phi_i\},\{\dot\phi_i\}) = T(\dot\phi) - U(\phi),
$$

in

- `T` comes from the capacitor network (corresponding to kinetic energy)
- `U` comes from inductance and Josephson potential energy (corresponding to potential energy)

Then by the conjugate momentum

$$
q_i = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot\phi_i}
$$

By establishing the Hamiltonian and quantizing it, the quantum model of the circuit can be obtained.

<Aside type="note" title="Historical context">

Write the circuit in Lagrangian/Hamiltonian form so that "circuit design" and "quantum system modeling" can use the same theoretical language.

</Aside>

## Project mapping

- Equivalent parameters (C, L, EJ) enter the model directly and are connected to the measurement frequency and coupling strength.
- Coordinate selection (node ​​flux / loop flux) will affect model simplicity and numerical stability.

## Limitations and approximations

- The lumped element approximation requires that the element size is much smaller than the relevant wavelength.
- Dispersion parameters and packaging effects require additional processing at high frequencies or large sizes.
