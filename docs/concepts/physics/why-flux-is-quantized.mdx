# Why is magnetic flux quantized?

## Summary of this chapter

In a superconducting closed loop, the allowed magnetic flux is not a continuous value, but an integer multiple of the flux quantum $\Phi_0 = h/2e$. This result arises directly from the single-valuedness of macroscopic wave functions and the gauge invariance of electromagnetic fields. This chapter starts from these two principles and gradually derives the conditions for magnetic flux quantization.

## Physical context

### 1. Starting from the macroscopic wave function

As mentioned in the previous chapter, the condensed state of superconductors can be described by order parameters (Tinkham, 2004, Ch. 4):

$$
\Psi(\mathbf{r}) = |\Psi(\mathbf{r})|\, e^{i\theta(\mathbf{r})}
$$

This wave function is continuous and single-valued throughout the superconductor - when it comes back to the origin along any closed path, the wave function must return to its original value. This is a basic requirement of the wave function in quantum mechanics.

### 2. What is canonical invariance?

In electromagnetism, the physical observables (electric field $\mathbf{E}$, magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$) are determined by Maxwell's equations. However, the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ and the scalar potential $\phi$ are not unique - the physics remains unchanged after the following **gauge transformation** (Jackson, 1999, Ch. 6):

$$
\mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{A} + \nabla\chi, \qquad
\phi \to \phi' = \phi - \frac{\partial \chi}{\partial t}
$$

where $\chi(\mathbf{r}, t)$ is any smooth function.

<Aside type="note" title="The physical meaning of gauge invariance">

Gauge invariance means that the potential itself is not a physical quantity, and only specific combinations of potentials (such as $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$) correspond to observable quantities. Therefore, any physical expression involving $\mathbf{A}$ must appear in gauge-invariant form.

</Aside>

### 3. Minimal coupling and gauge-invariant phase gradient

In quantum mechanics, the coupling of charged particles and electromagnetic fields is achieved through **minimal coupling**: the momentum operator $\hat{\mathbf{p}}$ is replaced by

$$
\hat{\mathbf{p}} \to \hat{\mathbf{p}} - e^*\mathbf{A}
$$

This is called Peierls substitution. For Cooper pair ($e^* = 2e$), the corresponding phase gradient in the superconducting current density is not simply $\nabla\theta$, but **gauge invariant phase gradient** (Tinkham, 2004, Ch. 4):

$$
\boldsymbol{\gamma}(\mathbf{r}) \equiv \nabla\theta - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\mathbf{A}
$$

<Aside type="tip" title="Why is this combination normatively invariant?">

When performing a gauge transformation $\mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{A} + \nabla\chi$, the phase of the wave function also changes: $\theta \to \theta + (2\pi / \Phi_0)\chi$ (this is a standard result of quantum mechanics). The changes in the two exactly offset:

$$
\nabla\theta' - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\mathbf{A}' = \left(\nabla\theta + \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\nabla\chi\right) - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\left(\mathbf{A} + \nabla\chi\right) = \nabla\theta - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\mathbf{A} = \boldsymbol{\gamma}
$$

Therefore $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ is invariant under gauge transformation and is a real physical quantity.

</Aside>

### 4. Uniqueness conditions on closed paths

Now consider integrating $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ along a closed path $C$ inside the superconductor:

$$
\oint_C \boldsymbol{\gamma} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \oint_C \left(\nabla\theta - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\mathbf{A}\right) \cdot d\mathbf{l}
$$

The left side is divided into two parts:

**Part 1**: $\oint_C \nabla\theta \cdot d\mathbf{l}$

The integral of phase $\theta$ along a closed path is equal to the total change in phase after one revolution. Since the wave function $\Psi = |\Psi| e^{i\theta}$ must be single-valued, $e^{i\theta}$ must return to its original value after one circle, which requires:

$$
\oint_C \nabla\theta \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 2\pi n, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}
$$

**Part 2**: $\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{l}$

According to Stokes' theorem:

$$
\oint_C \mathbf{A} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{A}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \Phi
$$

where $S$ is any surface bounded by $C$ and $\Phi$ is the total magnetic flux passing through the surface.

### 5. Magnetic flux quantization

Substituting the above results, we get:

$$
\oint_C \boldsymbol{\gamma} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = 2\pi n - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\Phi
$$

Deep within the bulk of a superconductor (outside the region within the London penetration depth $\lambda_L$), the superconducting current density approaches zero (a consequence of the Meissner effect). Since the superconducting current is proportional to $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$, this means:

$$
\boldsymbol{\gamma} = 0 \quad \text{(deep in superconductors)}
$$

Therefore integrating along a path $C$ located deep in the superconductor:

$$
0 = 2\pi n - \frac{2\pi}{\Phi_0}\Phi
$$

After sorting, the **magnetic flux quantization conditions** are obtained:

$$
\boxed{\Phi = n\,\Phi_0, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}}
$$

The magnetic flux passing through a superconducting closed loop can only be an integer multiple of the flux quantum $\Phi_0 = h/2e$.

<Aside type="note" title="Historical context">

Magnetic flux quantization was first observed in 1961 by two independent experimental teams: Deaver and Fairbank (1961) in the United States, and Doll and Näbauer (1961) in Germany. Their experiments also confirmed that the value of the magnetic flux quantum is $h/2e$ (not $h/e$), thus confirming that the carriers in superconductivity are pairs of electrons (Cooper pairs) rather than single electrons. This is one of the key experimental verifications of the BCS theory.

</Aside>

<Aside type="tip" title="From experiment to technology">

Magnetic flux quantization turns "superconducting is a macroscopic quantum state" into a directly measurable fact. It not only verifies the BCS theory, but also lays the physical foundation for SQUID magnetometers, quantum interferometers, and the entire superconducting quantum circuit technology.

</Aside>

## Project mapping

- $\Phi / \Phi_0$ directly determines the bias operating point of the SQUID loop and is the core parameter in circuit design.
- Quantization conditions impose constraints on the phases in the loop, limiting the allowed dynamical branches.
- In loops with Josephson junctions, the flux quantization condition is corrected to include the junction phase difference (see [SQUID Control of Nonlinear Inductance](squid-controls-nonlinear-inductance.mdx)).

## Limitations and approximations

- The above derivation assumes that path $C$ is completely deep in the superconductor bulk ($\boldsymbol{\gamma} = 0$). If the loop wall thickness is comparable to $\lambda_L$, a correction is required.
- Clear quantization can only be observed if the coherence length $\xi$ and temperature conditions allow.
- Loops with multiple Josephson junctions need to include phase jumps at the junctions ($\theta$ discontinuities on both sides of the junction).
